Prairie Creek Nature Center

Minimal site impact + a journey of experiences

Minimal site impact means more than not impacting the immediate site; it means lowering the embodied-energy of all materials used on a project as well as taking into consideration the lifespan of a structure. What will it accomplish? How will it impact its surroundings? In the case of the Prairie Creek Nature Center design proposition, the structure connects harmoniously with the wooded environment of its location on the Prairie Creek Reservoir in Muncie, IN. While the physical building serves as the focal point of the site, it is just one stop of many that visitors will encounter as they move through the woods and to the Prairie Creek Reservoir.



An elegant, lightweight lamella structure forms the outer shell. Within, the inhabitable spaces are suspended from the structure via thin cables. To achieve the desired lighting qualities within each interior space, individual wooded pieces of the lamella were removed, leaving wider gaps in the overall pattern. The interior spaces are composed of different enclosures based on program. For example, the top level, which functions as the exhibition and multipurpose space, is enclosed with glass so that it appears open to the lamella structure above. Hinged folding glass panels are located at either end of the structure to further enclose the interior space. In the warmer months, these panels remain open on the south side to force air through the space and allow for natural ventillation. The outermost layer consists of an ETFE skin, a material that allows the interior spaces to be shielded from the elements without inhibiting views in and out of the facility.




Structural explorations lead to discoveries in form that are otherwise impossible. This particular form was informed by the lamella structure. The interlocking members have astounding structural qualities that allow the delicate form of the Nature Center to take shape.



Three floors are suspended from the lamella structure so as not to disrupt the ground level ecology. The main floor is a direct extension of the pathway that travels through the site, and the upper two levels can be accessed via a system of suspended stairs and ramps.
